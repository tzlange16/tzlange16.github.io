The Organizational Evolution of Small Food Buying Clubs

My dissertation research revolved around the evolution of small food buying clubs, which are informal organizations of individuals who combine their purchasing power to obtain local and specialty goods in bulk. As globalized food production faces increased stress from a changing climate, small food buying clubs are one way to connect consumers to local growers and facilitate resiliance in the food system.

Preferences can differ among club members, leading to conflicts of interest where members have to help each other buy food they themselves may not want. My disseration explores how these groups overcome this dynamic, how club members' preferences change over time, and how these factors contribute to club operation.

The Dynamics of Cooperation in Co-operative Businesses

During my graduate tenure, our lab group did an evolutionary analysis on co-operative businesses. Co-operatives are businesses or organizations that are communally owned and operated, and they base their practices on 7 Co-operative principles:

  1. Voluntary & Open Membership
  2. Democratic Member Control
  3. Member Economic Participation
  4. Autonomy & Independence
  5. Education, Training, & Information
  6. Cooperation among Cooperatives
  7. Concern for Community

These organizations have smaler market share than share-holder and private organizations in their respective sectors, and they face ownership and use costs from conflicts of interest that typically arise in populations of agents with co-equal stake. Never the less, they endure as a business model. We analyzed these organizations in light of the science of cooperation and the organizational evolution, and found that enforcing behavioral cooperation through rules and institutions that encourage mutual goals and interpersonal reciprocity likely play a substantial role in their success

The Economic Impact of Recreational Fishing on the North American Great Lakes

In 2021, I was brought onto a project for Southwick Associates, who was working on estimating the economic impact of recreational anglers on the North American Great Lakes on a grant from the Great Lakes Fishery Commission. My contribution included breaking down the data from a large survey of anglers (n ≈ 10,000), and estimating their average daily expenditures. From there, I estimated the total expenditures of the estimated angler population, which my collegues fed into an IMPLAN input-output model to estimate the total impact of these expenditures across all 8 Great Lakes states. I am currently leading the peer-reviewed paper on these estimates for the Transactions of the American Fisheries Society

Notable Projects:

Variance of wall thickeness in Iroquois pottery. This client was a student looking for statistical help on behalf of their lab. Their group had gathered pottery fragments from several Iroquois sites in southern Onterio, and were interested in ascertaining which pots in their samples were cooking vessesls. They had one vessel that they knew was for cooking because there was abundant residue on the inner wall, but the rest of the vessels were less straightforward. During a whiteboard session, they described a prevailing theory in archeology that wall thickness consistency played a considerable part in the how ancient peoples chose their cooking pots, as uniform thickness increases durability and heats evenly. Working within this theory, we determined that the best course of action was to compare the variances of the vessels to the known cooking vessel, which helped us identify over half of the vessels as candidate cooking pots for further forensic examination.

Hypsographic Demography of the United States. This client was a documentary filmmaker investigating the effects of decreasing concentrations of atmospheric oxygen. They had already looked into the effects on fighter pilots, and was interested in looking into migration from higher altitudes. After looking into altitude sickness, they hypothesised that decreasing oxygen concentrations on top of thinner air may increase the incidence of complications resulting from altitude sickness, and this would cause individuals to migrate to lower altitudes. Following the methodology of Joel E. Cohen and Christopher Small, I calculated the average elevation of every county in the United States and combined it with census data from 1960-2010 to determine if there was a net migration away from higher altitudes at various age levels. Surpisingly enough, I found that the proportion of individuals living at higher altitudes had increased over the last 80 years, regardless of age cohort.
